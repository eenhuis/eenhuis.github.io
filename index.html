Managers create work. Engineers destroy work.

In general, managers (and I include TPMs in the manager bucket) think of things for engineers to do and engineers do them. Usually engineers build automated ways to do repeatable things and then managers can use the automation, but real outcomes usually only happen when engineers get involved.

If you think this is true, staffing a team with too many managers or only managers will result in two outcomes: 1) wasted ideas or 2) your team creating work for other teams. Creating work for other teams is generally a recipe for failure as we tend to run very lean. On my program teams, I have chosen to staff more engineering roles than in the past and I will add more as I can hire and develop the engineers I have. I think this has been an effective strategy.

Moreover, there is a dance that goes on at Amazon when you ask another team to do work. The sequence of events goes something like this:

Me: I want your team to do work. Other Person: How important is it to you? Me: Very Important. Together: Let's do it.

Note, this is rarely, if ever, the actual sequence of events. The actual second step is often disguised as "I will put it on my backlog", "Perform this intricate design procedure", "Come to this sequence of meetings", etc. The third step involves me proving the importance of the project somehow. By far, the most effective method I know is to dedicate my engineers to do the work. The dirty little secret is rarely does this actually involve my team doing any work. It merely signals that my project is "Very important", so important, I'm willing to do the work myself. Once the other team knows this, they will generally complete the task. By my estimation one in six times my team will actually have to do the work. The other five times, the team who owns the software will do the work.

Saturate, Incubate, Illuminate!

Everyone runs into "that” problem, the one that reveals itself at the worst times. How you handle and solve these problems will ultimately decide the outcome. Over my years as a manager I’ve had to deal with problems where the consequences were severe and costly. Some of these dealt with possible loss of life and others a million dollar mistake. Needless to say these decisions could not be made lightly.

Solving these problems requires creativity and follows a fundamental process. At the end of this process the answer can reveal itself and you feel empowered and reborn. I’ve outlined the four fundamental stages below.

First Insight

Sometimes finding the problem can be harder than discovering the answer. Other times the problem will present itself to you (usually at the most inopportune time). Solving these problems will take creative ideas and creative techniques. To think outside the box you need look at the problem from a new angle. As Albert Einstein put it, “We can’t solve problems by using the same kind of thinking we used when we created them.”

Saturation

Once you’ve identified your problem you need to reframe your mind. I like to use mind mapping to present ideas in a radial, graphical and non-linear manner. This helps the mind absorb the information and bake for a while. This stage is incredibly important, you need to see and hear all the information. Absorb as much information as possible so your mind can piece it all together.

Illumination

This is the Aha! Moment, the Eureka! Usually this comes at a time when your mind is at ease and free from thought. I usually get this moment in the middle of the night or when I’m running. When this wonderful moment arrives, it’s important to write down your thoughts and also how you got here (so you can do it again). Once you’ve written it down let it soak in, sometimes another thought will come into play as well. Verification

This is where the hard work comes in–taking your ideas to reality. This is where you create order out of your thoughts and tie everything together using the illuminated ideas. Check for clarity, brevity, and simplicity. Jon Ronson, author of Them: Adventures with Extremists encapsulates the process best. He says, “This is any writer’s greatest challenge: once you’ve chosen a story, investigate all the parameters, turn yourself insane, drown yourself in the details, then regain your sanity, and come out the other end with something …simple.”

Owning and Idea.

Availability, Latency, and Efficiency are not pieces of software. Availability, Latency, and Efficiency are ideas. These ideas need to be applied to other people's software. This implies that in order for me to be successful, other people are going to need to do work. I generally do not own these people's time. I am indirect. The only power I wield is influence. Influence is harder than just doing work. No one will convince me otherwise.

Approach your idea with as little bias as possible. Try and figure out what is actually happening by using data rather than opinion. Communicate facts not conjecture. This takes more effort than you think. It also helps to approach situations with humility. When you do not know, say you do not know. Then, go try and figure it out using data. This approach will gain your stakeholders trust in you. You will become their partner.

The Big Picture.

I’ve worked for multiple start-ups in the past, some successful and some not so successful. The common thread with successful start-ups is good managers and good leaders, but what does this mean? As Warren Bennis famously put it, “Managers are people who do things right and leaders are people who do the right thing.”

A lot of managers at start-ups are “big picture only” bosses, they see the long-term perspective, vision and setting a strategy. They view their work as generating big and vague ideas and leave the implementation and details as work done by the average worker. I’m a firm believer that if you can’t explain it you don’t understand it. More and more management is losing sight of the details, operations and glue that makes company stick.

Great managers are more than big thoughts; they understand the fundamentals of their industries. They are in tune with their organizations and teams. In Robert Sutton’s book Good Boss, Bad Boss he references the late Steve Jobs, who often visited the Apple Store near his house in Palo Alto (the first in this now large and extremely profitable chain). Jobs constantly fussed over details such as the quality of the shopping bags, where employees stood in the store, and the color of the walls and tables, and what they conveyed about the brand.

It’s this ability to dive deep into the details and still see the big picture that creates the best managers, and helps the best managers lead.
